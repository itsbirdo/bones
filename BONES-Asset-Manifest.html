Asset & Build Manifest

Unity native app · Persona-5-style flat 2D presentation · premium 3D reserved for the dice roll. Scope here is what assets exist, how they're authored, and how the dopamine machine is wired — not gameplay code.

0 · The one principle that organizes everything

2D is the world. 3D is the dice. Spend premium effort on the single moment the spec calls the heartbeat — pick-up → shake → one-die-at-a-time reveal → score. Everything else (street, wall, shadows, HUD, portraits, items, comic SFX, shop) is flat sprite/shader work that stays cheap, stylish, and on-model.

Outcome-first dice (locked this session). Game logic decides each die's face after applying procs, cheats, and the Squeeze. The roll is choreography to a known result — never a free sim that determines the outcome. Physics is presentation. This is what makes the sequential reveal, the lingering final die, and the visible-cheat moment possible at all.

1 · The dice — the only real 3D

1.1 Geometry

  • Base die: one rounded-cube mesh, reused everywhere. Topology is irrelevant (it never deforms) — model it, grab a free one, or AI-generate it, all fine.
  • Shape variants for rarity/upgrade tiers (distinct silhouettes = readability + collectibility). Keep to ~4–5 static meshes:
    • Standard cube — common
    • Heavier / worn-corner bevel — loaded / crooked
    • Faceted / gem-cut or elongated — trick / control (rare, precious)
    • Cracked / sigil-carved — curse

1.2 Surfaces — your wheelhouse (textures / materials)

One mesh + many material sets = the entire visible dice catalog.

  • Face texture set — hand-drawn inked pips on bone-white, matching the noir line. 6 faces, plus the crooked "tell" the spec wants (glint, heavier ink shadow), baked or shader-driven.
  • Body material — ink black, bone, gilded gold (Gilded Die), blood-marked (curse). Spot color carries meaning: amber = money/heat, red = danger.
  • Special faces: Two-Face (only two values exist); Lucky Six / Snake Killer / Set-Bone (normal faces, flagged for the cheat-tell shader on proc).

1.3 Authoring & import

  • Mesh → export FBX (or glTF) → drop in Assets/.
  • Face/body textures as PNG → assign to a custom toon/inked material.
  • A toon/ramp shader + outline beats realistic lighting here — it's what makes a 3D object sit convincingly on a 2D Sin City pavement. This is the single most important shader to get right.

1.4 The roll (outcome-first choreography)

  • Controlled tumble (recommended): procedural or short pre-baked tumble guaranteed to settle on the target face. Snappy, art-directable, dramatic — fits noir over simulation realism.
  • Alternative: physics-with-correction (let it roll, settle to target). More "real," less controllable.
  • Sequential reveal: dice resolve one at a time; the final die lingers. Trivial under outcome-first, near-impossible under free physics.
  • Visible cheat: on a loaded-die proc, play the tell (glint, mid-tumble face-flip, ink-pulse) — the same beat that ticks Suspicion.

3 · The dopamine machine Balatro / Cloverpit grade

What makes it feel incredible. None of it needs 3D beyond the dice — it's layered 2D effects, audio, timing, and escalation. Build it as a tiered, stackable feedback system so intensity scales with stake × Heat × proc.

3.1 Escalation tiers (the core Balatro/Cloverpit trick)

TierTrigger (example)What fires
0 — clackevery die landingsingle CLACK, dust puff, light haptic

The feel of escalation comes from tiers being clearly separated and earned — a high-Heat triple should look categorically different from a plain win, not just "more."

3.2 Reusable juice library

  • Number juice: payout counters that roll up with easing + overshoot; multiplier chips (×2.5) that stamp in and shake — the Balatro signature.
  • Particles: gold-coin burst, ink splatter, landing dust, smoke drift, Heat embers that intensify with the meter.
  • Screen effects: shake (scaled per tier), flash, ink/chromatic aberration on big hits, time-scale dip on the lingering final die.
  • Panel system: frame fractures into comic panels on streaks — a mask/overlay that subdivides the screen.
  • Audio layers: a music bed that adds stems as Heat climbs; diegetic clack/clink; tiered stingers. Adaptive audio does half the dopamine work — budget for it.
  • Haptics: tiered vibration (you have this now that you left WebGL). Tick per clack, rumble on jackpot.
  • Anticipation > payoff: spend timing budget on the pause before the reveal — the shake and the lingering final die — not only the celebration after.

3.3 Upgradable dice — making leveling feel good

Every upgrade die is %-proc + levels (1→3+) per the spec. Juice hooks:

  • Honing at the Fence gets its own detonation — sparks, the die "charging up," proc% ticking higher with a satisfying snap.
  • A proc mid-roll is a mini-celebration: die glows its identity color, cheat tell plays, a small LOADED! pops. Because procs are chance-based, each is a small win on top of the win.
  • Visual rarity tiers: shape + material + particle signature per tier (common → loaded → trick → curse) so a maxed crooked die looks dangerous and precious. Collectibility = dopamine.

3.4 Heat & Suspicion as feedback (tension is dopamine too)

  • Heat drives rising temperature: embers, warmer grade, music stems, faster shake. The screen feels hotter each consecutive win.
  • Suspicion is dread: red creeps in, crowd murmur rises, the meter pulses. The bust is the anti-jackpot — make it hit as hard negatively as a triple hits positively. Contrast makes both land.

4 · Audio asset list

  • Music: smoky upright jazz authored in stems/layers (base, +Heat1/2/3) for adaptive build. Vinyl crackle + rain ambience loop.
  • Diegetic SFX: dice clack on concrete (several variants), coin clink, fist-shake rattle, cup pickup.
  • Stingers: win, big win (4-5-6), jackpot (triple), the bust needle-scratch/bass-drop, honing snap, proc cue.
  • UI: taps, drawer swipes, wager stepper ticks.
  • Format: WAV for short SFX, compressed (OGG/MP3) for music. Set Unity load types accordingly (decompress-on-load for tiny SFX, streaming for music).

5 · Providing assets to Unity (mechanics)

  1. Drop the file into Assets/ — Unity auto-imports.
  2. Configure import settings per type: Sprite → Sprite (2D and UI), set PPU, pack into atlases; die FBX → no rig, assign toon material; audio → load type per §4; fonts → generate TextMeshPro assets from .ttf/.otf (one condensed display face, one clean numeric face).
  3. Lock a folder structure & naming convention up front and make the AI follow it. Suggested: Assets/Art/{Dice,World,UI,SFX_Letters,Portraits}, Assets/Audio/{Music,SFX,Stingers}, Assets/Materials, Assets/Prefabs, Assets/Scripts, Assets/Scenes.

6 · Suggested build order (prove the feel first)

  1. One die, one material, controlled tumble, flat plate — die lands on a logic-chosen face with a CLACK and a blob shadow. The whole game in miniature.
  2. The reveal sequence — multiple dice, one at a time, lingering final die, basic SFX-letter pop.
  3. Tiered juice (0→3) — the escalation system + number juice. Here it starts to feel like Balatro.
  4. Cee-lo scoring + Heat — banker logic, payout × Heat, streak temperature.
  5. Suspicion + bust — the dread counter and the anti-jackpot.
  6. 3–4 upgrade dice + honing + cheat tell — proc juice, visual rarity, the Fence.
  7. A short run — nights, escalating collections, lose-if-broke/whacked.

Get steps 1–3 feeling incredible on a real mid-range phone before building breadth.

7 · AI: reliable vs verify

  • Reliable: gameplay/scoring logic, the juice/tier state machine, number-juice tweening, UI wiring, audio triggering, outcome-first roll logic.
  • Verify carefully: the toon/inked shader and compositing 3D dice on 2D, the Animator/tumble setup, camera/lighting for the dice layer, sprite-atlas/draw-call performance on device. The spots AI is shakiest — where your senior eye pays off.
